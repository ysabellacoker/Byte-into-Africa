Ghana

Ghanaian cuisine is a celebration of bold flavors, rich spices, and comforting textures. From bustling street food stalls to traditional home kitchens, the food of Ghana reflects a deep heritage and a love for hearty, satisfying meals. Whether it’s a savory porridge or a crunchy snack, Ghanaian dishes are full of character and warmth, making them a delicious window into the country’s culture.

Appetizers

Start your journey through Ghanaian food with these delightful appetizers. From crunchy peanut snacks to spicy porridge and sweet fried dough, these dishes are the perfect introduction to Ghana’s bold and vibrant flavor palette.

Kuli Kuli

Kuli Kuli are crunchy peanut-based snacks that are incredibly popular across Ghana and other parts of West Africa. Made from ground peanuts and spices, they're a tasty and nutritious alternative to chips or roasted nuts. These golden bites are perfect with a drink or as a quick snack any time of day.

Ingredients

  • 8 oz. salted, dry roasted peanuts
  • 1 tablespoon grated fresh ginger
  • ¼ teaspoon ground chili pepper
  • Peanut oil (for frying)

Instructions

Grind the peanuts and ginger together until smooth. Mix in the ground chili pepper and blend thoroughly. Squeeze the paste to extract as much oil as possible—this step is crucial for a crispy result. Shape the paste into about 20 pieces—sticks, small rings, or balls work great. If the mixture feels dry, add a couple tablespoons of water to help with shaping. Heat a deep pan of peanut oil and fry the kuli kuli on medium heat until they’re a nice golden brown. Drain on paper towels and serve warm or cooled.

Bofrot

Bofrot, sometimes called puff-puff, is a beloved Ghanaian treat that doubles as breakfast, dessert, or snack. These lightly sweetened, nutmeg-spiced dough balls are deep-fried until golden and fluffy. Bofrot is enjoyed plain or dressed up with toppings like powdered sugar or chocolate spread.

Ingredients

  • 4 2/3 cups (575g) all-purpose flour
  • 1 cup (200g) sugar, divided
  • 1½ teaspoons nutmeg
  • 1½ teaspoons salt
  • 1 tablespoon (1 packet) active dry or instant yeast
  • 3 teaspoons vanilla extract
  • 1 can (354ml) evaporated milk
  • 1¼ cups warm water (divided: ¾ cup, ¼ cup, ¼ cup)
  • Oil for frying

Instructions

If using active dry yeast, mix ¾ cup of warm water with a teaspoon of sugar and the yeast in a bowl. Let it sit for 5–10 minutes until foamy. In a large bowl, combine the flour, the rest of the sugar, salt, nutmeg, and instant yeast (if not using active dry yeast). Add the foamy yeast mixture, followed by evaporated milk. Mix the vanilla with ½ cup water and pour in, then stir to form a thick, stretchy batter. Traditionally mixed by hand, but a whisk or mixer works too. Cover the bowl with plastic wrap and let it rise in a warm place for 2 hours until doubled in size.

After rising, gently deflate the dough. Heat oil in a deep pot. Scoop small portions of dough (using your hand or an ice cream scoop) and carefully drop into the oil. Fry in batches, cooking each side for about 4–5 minutes or until golden brown all over. Turn continuously using a chopstick or skewer to ensure even browning. Serve warm with tea, porridge, or sweet toppings.

Hausa Koko

Hausa Koko is a warm, slightly sour porridge made from fermented corn or millet dough, infused with spices like ginger and cloves. It’s often served for breakfast and offers a comforting kick from chili powder. Its tangy taste may be new to some, but it’s a favorite staple in many Ghanaian homes.

Ingredients

  • ½ cup fermented corn or millet dough
  • 3 cups water (divided)
  • Pinch of kosher salt
  • ¼ teaspoon ground cloves
  • ½ teaspoon ground ginger
  • ¼ teaspoon hot chili powder
  • Sugar to taste (optional)

Instructions

Place the fermented dough in a pot and mix with 1 cup of cold water until smooth. Place over high heat and bring to a gentle simmer while stirring. Gradually add 2 cups of boiling water and continue stirring. Lumps may form—use a whisk to smooth them out. Stir in the salt, ground cloves, ginger, and chili powder. Let it simmer on low heat for about 10 minutes. Sweeten with sugar if desired, and serve hot.

Main Dishes

Ghanaian main courses are rich, filling, and full of bold flavors. These dishes often center around hearty ingredients like yam, rice, and cassava, accompanied by savory stews or flavorful soups. Whether it's a festive plate of Jollof rice or a warming bowl of peanut soup with fufu, these recipes represent the heart of Ghanaian culinary tradition.

Peanut Soup with Fufu

Groundnut soup is a deeply comforting dish enjoyed across Ghana, often served with fufu. Made from blended peanuts (or peanut butter), this spicy, rich soup can be customized with a variety of proteins like chicken, beef, or goat.

For the Soup

  • 400g groundnut paste or peanut butter
  • 1.8 kg whole chicken, cut into pieces
  • 1 medium onion, chopped
  • 1 large onion, blended
  • 7 scotch bonnets
  • 3 tbsp ginger, blended
  • 3 large tomatoes
  • 2 tbsp tomato paste
  • Salt
  • Basil

Instructions

Mix peanut butter with 1L of water, 1 tbsp tomato paste, 3 crushed scotch bonnets, and chopped onions. Cook over low-medium heat for ~20 minutes until thickened and oil appears. Add 3L of water to the mixture and strain into a large pot. Boil for 30 minutes, then simmer. Add whole tomatoes, 1 tbsp tomato paste, and 4 scotch bonnets. Steam chicken with ginger, half the blended onions, scotch bonnets, basil, and salt. Add chicken and broth to the soup. Blend tomatoes and scotch bonnets, strain, and return to pot. Add remaining onions and simmer for 60 minutes. Serve hot with fufu.

For the Fufu

  • 1 medium cassava tuber
  • 2 green plantains
  • Water to cover
  • 1 tsp salt (optional)

Instructions

Peel and slice cassava and plantain. Boil for ~30 mins until tender. Drain and blend in a food processor, adding water as needed until smooth and stretchy. For the traditional method, pound plantain and cassava separately, then combine and mold into balls. Serve warm with soup.

Tips

  • Always cook cassava thoroughly—raw cassava is toxic.
  • To shape fufu into a ball, use wet hands or wrap in plastic and twist.

Ghanaian Jollof Rice

Ghanaian Jollof is a flavorful one-pot rice dish with a rich tomato base and a blend of spices. It’s a favorite for parties, holidays, and everyday meals alike.

Ingredients

  • 1 large white onion, diced
  • 5 cloves garlic, shredded
  • 1/4 cup grated fresh ginger
  • 3 large tomatoes, sliced
  • 1/2 cup vegetable or canola oil
  • 2 tsp Jamaican curry powder
  • 1 can (29 oz) tomato sauce
  • 1 hot pepper (Habanero or Scotch Bonnet)
  • 1½ tsp ground cayenne pepper
  • 1½ tsp salt
  • 1 cup water
  • 1 cup chicken stock
  • 2 cups rinsed basmati or jasmine rice

Instructions

Cook onions in oil until translucent. Add garlic, ginger, and curry powder; stir for 3 minutes. Add tomatoes and cook until soft. Stir in tomato sauce, whole pepper, cayenne, and salt. Simmer gently for up to 2 hours, stirring occasionally until oil separates. In a new pot, combine 2 cups of this sauce with broth and water. Bring to a boil, stir in rice, reduce heat, and cover. Cook for 15–20 minutes or until rice is done, adding extra sauce or liquid if needed. Avoid breaking the whole pepper while cooking.

Yam Pottage (Mpoto-Mpoto)

This comforting one-pot meal is made with yam, peppers, fish, and palm oil. Also known as yam porridge, it's thick, flavorful, and satisfying—perfect for lunch or dinner.

Ingredients

  • 1 medium yam tuber
  • 1 red bell pepper
  • 1 large tomato
  • 1 habanero pepper
  • 1 large onion, divided
  • 3 cups water
  • 1/2 cup fresh palm oil
  • 2 tbsp crayfish
  • Salt to taste
  • 2 tsp chicken bouillon or 2 cubes
  • 15 oz mackerel (or salmon, dried fish, stockfish)
  • Leafy greens: basil, spinach, kale, or ugwu

Instructions

Peel and cut yam into chunks, rinse until clear. Blend pepper, tomato, habanero, and half the onion. In a pot, combine yam, blended sauce, salt, bouillon, and water. Cook 10 minutes. Stir in palm oil, sliced onions, and crayfish. Cook another 10 minutes. Mash some yam for texture. Add fish and simmer 3–5 minutes. Stir in leafy greens and serve hot.

Desserts and Drinks

Ghanaian desserts reflect the simplicity and richness of the country's culinary heritage. Made with staple ingredients like groundnuts and coconut, these treats offer satisfying sweetness with textures ranging from crunchy to chewy. Whether it’s the nutty nkate cake or the coconut-packed kube, these recipes are beloved snacks for all ages.

Nkate Cake (Peanut Brittle)

Nkate cake is a crunchy Ghanaian snack made from just two ingredients—groundnuts and sugar. Known in Twi as “nkateɛ” (peanuts), this sweet is similar to peanut brittle, but with a simpler process and fewer ingredients. It’s a favorite treat sold in markets and by street vendors, offering a perfect blend of nuttiness and caramel crunch.

Ingredients

  • 200 grams groundnuts or peanuts (unsalted, roasted, and peeled)
  • 200 grams granulated sugar
  • 1 tablespoon neutral-tasting vegetable oil (for greasing the board and rolling pin)

Instructions

  1. Crush the groundnuts into smaller pieces using a rolling pin or pulse briefly in a food processor.
  2. Grease a wooden board and rolling pin with oil to prevent sticking.
  3. In a heavy-bottomed pan, melt the sugar over low heat until it turns golden and caramelized.
  4. Quickly stir in the crushed groundnuts until well combined.
  5. Transfer the mixture onto the greased board and shape it into a rectangle while still hot.
  6. Cut into diagonal pieces using a sharp knife and let cool completely before serving.

Kube (Coconut Candy)

Kube, which means coconut in Fante, is a chewy, sweet Ghanaian treat made from grated coconut and caramelized sugar. This delightful candy is shaped into small balls and often enjoyed during festive occasions or as an everyday snack.

Ingredients

  • 1 mature coconut (kube dwe)
  • ½ cup sugar
  • ¼ cup (60ml) water
  • 1 teaspoon lemon juice

Instructions

  1. Crack the coconut, remove the shell, wash, and grate it to your preferred size. Spread grated coconut on a towel to dry slightly.
  2. In a heavy-bottom saucepan, mix sugar, water, and lemon juice. Cook over medium heat until golden in color.
  3. Reduce heat and stir in the grated coconut. Continue stirring until the mixture slightly browns.
  4. Remove from heat and place the saucepan in cold water to cool and stop cooking.
  5. Dip a spoon in cold water and scoop the mixture onto a plate to form candy balls. Let cool completely before serving or storing in an airtight container.
