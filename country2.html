Ethiopia

Ethiopian cuisine is a vibrant celebration of bold spices, hearty textures, and deep-rooted traditions. Meals are often shared, bringing people together over Injera and a variety of richly flavored stews and snacks. Whether you're enjoying a crisp Sambusa or a warm plate of Qategna, each dish tells a story of heritage and hospitality.

Appetizers

Start your culinary journey with these bold and flavorful bites that awaken the senses.

Sambusa

  • 1 phyllo dough sheet per sambusa (frozen, thawed)
  • Olive oil
  • 1/4 cup dried lentils
  • 1 tbsp chopped onions
  • 1 tsp berbere spice mix (see below)
  • Juice of 1 lime
  • 1/2 tsp dried ginger (or fresh grated)
  • Salt, to taste

Instructions

  1. Preheat your oven to 375°F (190°C).
  2. Cook the lentils according to the package instructions. Once tender, mash them slightly and mix with onions, berbere, ginger, lime juice, and salt. Taste and adjust seasoning as needed.
  3. Place a sheet of phyllo dough on a flat surface and lightly brush it with olive oil. Carefully fold it into thirds lengthwise.
  4. Spoon about two tablespoons of filling near the edge, about an inch from the bottom, then fold the dough into a triangle shape, continuing until the sheet is fully wrapped.
  5. Seal the final fold with a touch of olive oil and place sambusas on a baking sheet.
  6. Bake for 30–35 minutes or until golden and crispy.
Note: Berbere spice is the heart of Ethiopian flavor—see how to make it below!

Homemade Berbere Spice Mix

  • 1/4 cup dried red chilis (de-seeded for less heat)
  • 1/4 tsp fenugreek seeds
  • 1 tbsp paprika
  • 1/4 tsp garam masala
  • 1 tsp cumin powder
  • 1 tsp coriander powder
  • 1/4 tsp cinnamon
  • 1/4 tsp allspice
  • 1/4 tsp cloves
  • 1/4 tsp whole black pepper
  • 1/2 tsp onion powder
  • 1/4 tsp garlic powder
  • 1/2 tsp ginger powder
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1/4 tsp dried sacred basil powder (optional)

Grind everything together in a spice grinder. Store in an airtight jar and use within a couple of weeks for the best flavor.

Dabo Kolo

Dabo Kolo is a crunchy and spicy snack enjoyed in both Ethiopia and the Democratic Republic of the Congo. It’s perfect for sharing and packed with toasty goodness.

  • 200g barley
  • 100g red chickpeas
  • 100g white chickpeas
  • 25g Ethiopian sunflower seeds
  • 50g sesame seeds
  • 30g peanuts (optional)
  • 1 tbsp Berbere spice
  • 2 tbsp olive oil

Instructions

  1. Wash the barley thoroughly, then boil it with a pinch of salt for about 5 minutes. Drain the water and toast the barley over low heat until golden. Set aside.
  2. Boil the chickpeas with a bit of salt for 10 minutes, then drain. Toast them over low heat until dry and crunchy.
  3. In the same pan, toast the peanuts, sunflower seeds, and sesame seeds separately over low heat until they release aroma and change color slightly.
  4. Combine all the toasted ingredients in a large bowl.
  5. In a small bowl, mix olive oil and Berbere spice together. Pour over the toasted mixture and mix everything by hand until evenly coated.
  6. Enjoy fresh, or store in an airtight jar for snacking later!

Qategna (Kategna)

Qategna is a simple yet flavorful Ethiopian snack made from Injera soaked in spiced butter and berbere. It’s typically enjoyed warm for breakfast or a light meal and brings warmth and comfort with every bite.

  • 1–2 pieces of freshly baked hot Injera
  • 2 tbsp berbere
  • 2 tbsp Te’smi/Niter Kibbeh (spiced clarified butter)
  • Salt to taste

Instructions

  1. Reserve your last Injera from a fresh batch—let it cook a little longer to give it extra crispness.
  2. Place the Injera on a large plate while still hot.
  3. Spread the berbere and Niter Kibbeh across the Injera using your hands or a spoon. If it’s too hot, mix the butter and berbere first in a pan on low heat, then spoon it over the Injera.
  4. Sprinkle a bit of salt to enhance the flavors.
  5. Fold the Injera to keep it warm and soft, then serve immediately with a cup of hot tea.
Did you know? Qategna is often made at home using the last Injera from the pan. It’s a comforting dish that highlights Ethiopia’s tradition of communal eating and connection through food.

Main Dishes

Explore traditional Ethiopian dishes that offer bold flavors, rich history, and cultural depth. From the famous Doro Wat to the quick and spicy Shekla Tibs, these recipes will give you a true taste of Ethiopia!

Doro Wat

Doro Wat is Ethiopia's national dish, a rich, spicy chicken stew that’s full of flavor. It’s traditionally served with injera, which is used to scoop up the stew. This dish packs a punch with its heat and is perfect for those who love bold flavors.

Ingredients

  • 3 lbs chicken thighs (cut into 1-inch pieces) or chicken breasts (cut into ½-inch pieces)
  • 2 tablespoons lemon juice
  • 2 tablespoons niter kibbeh (spiced clarified butter)
  • 2 tablespoons olive oil
  • 3 cups yellow onions (finely minced or pureed)
  • 3 tablespoons butter
  • 1 tablespoon minced garlic
  • 1 tablespoon minced ginger
  • 1/4 cup Ethiopian berbere spice mix
  • 1 1/2 teaspoons salt
  • 1/2 cup Tej (or white wine with honey)
  • 1 cup chicken stock
  • 4 hard-boiled eggs

Instructions

Start by marinating the chicken with lemon juice for 30 minutes. Heat the niter kibbeh and olive oil in a Dutch oven. Add the onions and sauté on low heat for 45 minutes, stirring occasionally. Then, add garlic, ginger, and 1 tablespoon butter. Cook for another 20 minutes. Add the berbere spice and remaining butter, and cook for an additional 30 minutes. Add the marinated chicken, chicken stock, salt, and Tej or white wine with honey. Bring to a boil, then lower the heat and simmer for 45 minutes. Finally, add the hard-boiled eggs and cook on low for 15 minutes. Serve hot with injera, rice, or bread.

Shekla Tibs

Shekla Tibs is a spicy and aromatic stir-fried beef dish with a burst of flavor. It’s fast to make and brings together traditional Ethiopian spices for a meal that's quick, delicious, and full of warmth.

Ingredients

  • 1 large red onion (sliced thinly)
  • 1/4 cup ghee
  • 2 lbs beef (cut into bite-sized pieces)
  • 2 tablespoons berbere spice mix
  • 1 teaspoon ground fenugreek
  • 1/2 teaspoon cardamom (optional)
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground ginger
  • 1/4 teaspoon cumin
  • 1/4 teaspoon ground clove
  • 1 teaspoon black pepper
  • 3-4 garlic cloves (sliced thinly)
  • 2 cups whole peeled tomatoes (broken into bits)
  • 1-5 green chilies (jalapenos or serranos)
  • 1/2 cup red wine

Instructions

Heat a sauté pan on medium-high and fry the onions until the edges start to char. Add ghee and beef to the pan and stir-fry on high heat until the beef is browned on the outside, keeping it rare inside. Add spices, garlic, and chilies, and fry for another 30 seconds. Stir in the tomatoes and wine, cooking for 1-2 minutes. Serve immediately.

Asa Wot and Injera

Asa Wot is a flavorful fish stew that's a popular Ethiopian dish. When paired with injera, a spongy flatbread, it becomes a complete meal with a balance of savory and tangy flavors.

Ingredients for Asa Wot

  • 500 grams of monkfish or white fish fillets
  • 3 limes
  • A knob of ginger
  • 3 medium-sized onions
  • 4 medium-sized tomatoes
  • 6 garlic cloves
  • 4-6 tablespoons of berbere spice
  • 5 tablespoons cold-pressed rapeseed oil
  • Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper

Instructions for Asa Wot

Marinate the fish with ginger, lime zest, and juice for at least 30 minutes. In a saucepan, heat the oil, and sauté onions until soft. Add garlic, ginger, and chopped tomatoes. Cook for 5 minutes, then add berbere spice and cook for 20 minutes. Add the fish, simmer for 10 minutes, then adjust salt to taste and serve with lime slices.

Ingredients for Injera

  • 160 grams teff flour
  • 230 grams all-purpose flour
  • 250 grams sourdough starter
  • 945 grams water
  • 1 tbsp baking powder
  • 1 tbsp cornstarch

Instructions for Injera

Blend teff flour and water, then add all-purpose flour and sourdough starter. Blend again and allow the batter to rest for 15 minutes. Heat a non-stick skillet on high, pour the batter, and cook until holes form. Cover, cook until the edges curl, and then transfer to a clean cloth to cool. Repeat with the remaining batter.

Desserts and Drinks

Delight in these sweet treats from Ethiopia! Whether you're craving something light and fluffy like Mandazi or a spiced, buttery snack like Chiko, these desserts are perfect for any occasion.

Mandazi

Mandazi, also known as African donuts, are deliciously light, fluffy, and infused with aromatic cardamom and cinnamon. These fried pastries are perfect for breakfast, as a snack, or paired with your favorite tea or coffee. Enjoy them plain or dusted with powdered sugar.

Ingredients

  • 1 teaspoon active dry yeast
  • ½ cup warm water (about 110°F)
  • 3 cups all-purpose flour
  • ½ teaspoon salt
  • 1 teaspoon cardamom
  • 1 teaspoon cinnamon
  • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
  • 1 large egg
  • ½ cup coconut milk
  • ½ cup sugar
  • Vegetable oil (for frying)

Instructions

In a small bowl, combine the yeast and warm water. Stir and let sit for 5 minutes until the yeast dissolves. In the bowl of a mixer, add flour, salt, cardamom, and cinnamon. Mix well, then add vegetable oil, egg, coconut milk, sugar, and the yeast mixture. Using the dough hook, knead until the dough is smooth and no longer sticky, adding extra flour if necessary.

Cover the dough with plastic wrap and let it rise for about 1 hour. Once it has risen, divide the dough into 6 portions. Roll each piece out to about 1 cm (less than ½ inch) thick and cut into triangles or your preferred shape.

Heat 2-3 inches of vegetable oil in a deep pan to 350°F. If you don't have a thermometer, drop in a small piece of dough—if it sizzles and floats, the oil is ready. Fry the dough in batches, ensuring not to overcrowd the pan, and cook until golden brown on both sides. Transfer the cooked Mandazi to a plate lined with paper towels to absorb excess oil. Repeat with the remaining dough.

Once all Mandazi are fried, sprinkle them with powdered sugar and enjoy!

Chiko

Chiko is a traditional Ethiopian treat made from Beso Duket (barley flour) combined with spiced Niter Kibbeh (clarified butter). It's a simple yet flavorful snack, rich in protein and perfect with Ethiopian coffee or tea.

Ingredients

  • 2 cups Beso Duket (barley flour)
  • 1 teaspoon caraway seeds
  • 1 teaspoon ground coriander
  • 1 teaspoon ground cardamom
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1 cup melted Niter Kibbeh (Ethiopian clarified butter)

Instructions

In a bowl, mix together the Beso Duket, caraway seeds, ground coriander, cardamom, and salt. Gradually add the melted Niter Kibbeh to the dry ingredients, stirring continuously until smooth and free of lumps.

Transfer the mixture to a flat pan or container (a 32-ounce rectangular plastic container works well). Press the dough evenly into the container, ensuring the corners are filled. Cover with plastic wrap and refrigerate for at least 1 hour or until firm.

Once chilled, remove the dough from the fridge and cut it into squares, triangles, or any small shapes of your choice. Enjoy these rich, buttery bites alongside a cup of Ethiopian coffee or tea.
